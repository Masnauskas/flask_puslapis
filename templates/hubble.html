Hubble Space telescope
Launched in 1990, Hubble has been visited by astronauts four times in order to make repairs and add new instruments. Each instrument that flies on Hubble has special features that let astronomers study the heavens in different ways. Hubble's unique capabilities can also be partnered with other space observatories and those on the ground to enable scientists to explore the universe in ways that no single mission could ever accomplish alone.

Hubble Space telescope programs
Over its long service history the Hubble observatory has made many discoveries. Probably the most famous one is a picture of interstellar gas and dust in the Eagle Nebula, in the Serpens constellation, some 6,500–7,000 light-years (2,000–2,100 pc; 61–66 Em) from Earth. The photograph is usually called the Pillars of Creation. Hubble took many images. Some of them can be seen below.

Hubble Space telescope programs
Since the start of the program, a number of research projects have been carried out, some of them almost solely with Hubble. Although the Hubble observatory is nearing the end of its life, there are still major projects scheduled for it. One example is the current (2022) ULLYSES project (Ultraviolet Legacy Library of Young Stars as Essential Standards) which will last for three years to observe a set of high- and low-mass young stars and will shed light on star formation and composition.

Introducing Hubble's ULLYSES Program
For centuries, astronomers have studied the stars, gathering information about their precise positions and compositions to learn how they form. But it wasn't until telescopes were launched into space that we could begin to capture some of the most important information - which is delivered by ultraviolet light.
